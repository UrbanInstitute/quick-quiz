Stoic Ethics: A Summary

The Ancient Art of Not Caring

Stoicism is a relatively older method of thinking, tracing its roots all the way back to the Hellenistic period. The majority of teachings we have for this philosophy comes from writers of the Imperial era, namely Seneca, Epictetus, and Marcus Aurelius, and luckily for us, these teachings focus primarily on ethics.

The original Stoics viewed this philosophy not merely as just a code of ethics, but rather, as a way of life. The philosophy itself is based solely in logic, and how to logically view the world around oneself. The path to happiness (or as they call it, eudaimonia) reveals itself to one who is not controlled by the humanistic desires of pleasure or fears of pain, they simply find their role in the world and perform that. To Stoics, virtue acts as the sole good in the world; everything else is just not nearly as important. Therefore, if virtue is the only good, then corporations should be locking down their social responsibility, as any virtuous person should, but we can see that this is not always the case.
