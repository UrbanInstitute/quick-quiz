Utilitarian Ethics: A Summary

A bit about the history of utilitarianism, it can be traced back to as early as the 17th century, to ‘theological’ utilitarians such as Richard Cumberland and John Gay. These men started the conversation about the nature of virtue and how it pertains to life itself. Ultimately, this led to the discussion about the outcomes of such virtues and also vices, and how it should be made necessary that virtue should be maximized above the rest, considering that would result in the best outcomes. Classical utilitarianism, prompted by Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill, focused more on hedonistic value theory (focusing solely on actions that produced pleasure or pain), but in more recent times, has shifted to more a pure consequentialist view.

In short, the ideal ethical choice using this framework will always be the one that generates the most good for the maximum number of people possible. It’s a simple calculus; every action has costs or benefits, so which one will yield the most benefit? A potential danger comes in, however, when utilitarianism is used to focus on issues such as justice or individual rights. It’s easy to make mathematical decisions when stakes are low, but for example, in the situation shown in the example photo above, it’s clear there’s no easy decision to be made there, because either way, people will die. However, because this framework can sometimes be reduced down into simply mathematics, it’s suitable for discussing corporate social responsibility.
